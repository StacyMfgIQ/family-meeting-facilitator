Part One · Still just you

What do you actually need?

Not what's reasonable. Not what you think people can give. What you actually need. Select everything that's true.

📅
A full day off
Not a short visit. Not a "I'll check in." A full day where you don't have to think about any of it.
📆
Reliable weekly help — on a schedule
Something you can count on. Not "I'll try to come by." An actual recurring commitment.
🏥
Help with medical appointments
Scheduling, driving, sitting in the appointment, following up on what was said. All of it.
💰
Financial contribution or shared costs
Care costs money. That burden shouldn't fall on one person just because they're closest.
✅
Ownership of one ongoing task
Not help. Ownership. One person takes full responsibility for one thing — and it's done.
💬
To be believed when I say I'm struggling
Without having to justify it. Without someone saying "but you're so good at this."
🆘
A real emergency backup person
Someone I can call at the last minute, without guilt, and know they will actually come.
🫂
Someone to ask how I am — and mean it
And wait for the real answer. Not "you're amazing, I don't know how you do it" and then leave.
🙏
Recognition that this has a cost
To my health. My work. My relationships. My own life. Not just gratitude — real recognition.

Part Two · The Family Meeting

Your meeting agenda

These conversation topics were built from what you shared. Each one comes with language you can use — so you don't have to find the words in the moment when emotions are high.

💡 A note on how to run this meeting
Start by reading the opening statement out loud. Then go through each topic. The goal isn't to assign blame — it's to end with a written list of who does what, by when. Vague commitments don't count. "I'll help more" is not an answer. A name, a task, and a date is an answer.
Open the meeting with this
"I called this meeting because I love our family and I love [parent's name]. And I need things to change — not because anyone is bad, but because the way things are right now isn't sustainable for me. I'm going to share some specific things. I need you to hear them without getting defensive. At the end, I need us to leave with actual commitments — not feelings, but names, tasks, and dates. That's the only outcome I need from today."

Part Three · After the Meeting

Record what was actually agreed

This is the most important step. Vague goodwill fades. Written commitments don't. Fill this in during or right after the meeting — while everyone can still see it.

⚠️ The empty commitment problem
Research consistently shows that commitments without a name, a specific task, and a deadline are not commitments — they're intentions. Intentions feel good in the moment and disappear by Tuesday. Require specificity. It's not unkind. It's the only thing that actually works.
